# PATTERN-REF-BEAR-FLAG-CONTINUATION

## Status
Reference document for the intended V1 continuation-pattern scope.
This file is not runtime proof.

## Pattern identity
- Canonical id: `BEAR_FLAG_CONTINUATION`
- Domain label: `BearFlagContinuation`
- Family: continuation pattern
- Bias: bearish after confirmation
- Typical context: sharp decline followed by a short, orderly upward or sideways correction

## Core reading
A bear flag is the inverse continuation reading of a bull flag.
It usually contains:
- a strong downward impulse, often called the flagpole
- a slower counter-trend consolidation, often drifting slightly upward or sideways, called the flag

The standard interpretation is that selling pressure dominated the impulse and that the rebound reflects temporary consolidation rather than a true bullish reversal.

## Structural conditions
A pattern candidate should not be treated as a bear flag unless all of the following are materially present:
- a meaningful impulsive decline exists first
- the post-impulse consolidation is shorter and less aggressive than the pole
- the consolidation slopes modestly upward or stays relatively flat
- the consolidation does not fully negate the preceding bearish impulse
- the pattern still reads as a pause in weakness rather than a full structural bottoming process

## Situations to distinguish
### Clean bearish continuation case
A strong downside pole followed by an orderly rebound supports the classic bear-flag reading.

### Weak impulse or oversized rebound case
The pattern thesis becomes weaker.
A deep rebound can mean the market is doing more than pausing.

### No clear prior selloff
Do not force a bear-flag interpretation.
The prior impulsive decline is central to the pattern identity.

## Detection reading
Useful observable facts:
- visible impulse leg lower
- contained rebound or sideways pause
- consolidation channel or mini-range against the prior move
- selling pressure cools temporarily without a full trend reversal

## Validation reading
A bear flag becomes materially stronger when:
- price closes below flag support
- breakout direction matches the prior bearish impulse
- breakout is accompanied by stronger volume or other confirming evidence
- price holds below the flag instead of rebounding back inside it

## Invalidation reading
A continuation reading weakens or fails when:
- price breaks above flag resistance
- rebound becomes too deep or too strong relative to the pole
- downside breakout attempt fails and price re-enters the flag
- momentum evidence no longer supports a bearish continuation thesis

## Target logic
A common educational measuring rule is to project the flagpole height downward from the breakout level.
That remains a reference projection only.

## Risk and advice guidance
### When the flag is still forming
Advice should remain cautious and conditional.
The bearish idea exists, but confirmation is not yet present.

### When downside breakout occurs
Helpful guidance usually focuses on:
- confirming a close beneath flag support
- checking whether breakout quality is supported by stronger volume
- monitoring whether the move holds below the broken boundary

### When price breaks above flag resistance
Advice should shift toward invalidation or at least strong caution.
That move conflicts with a confirmed bear-flag continuation reading.

## Common mistakes to avoid
- labelling any rebound during a decline as a bear flag
- ignoring whether the preceding selloff was truly impulsive
- ignoring volume context
- entering before confirmation
- confusing bottoming behavior with a still-valid continuation flag

## V1 wording guardrails
- Do not call the pattern confirmed while price remains inside the flag.
- Do not ignore resistance failure above the flag.
- Do not present projected downside as certainty.
- Do not preserve a bear-flag label after a structurally invalidating recovery.
